Why gross margin lies when capacity is tight

Conventional accounting says: rank products by gross margin, focus on the high-margin ones. That works when capacity is unlimited. When one resource is the bottleneck, the right ranking is throughput per minute of that resource - not gross margin per unit.

A 60%-margin product that takes 45 minutes on the bottleneck earns less per bottleneck minute than a 35%-margin product that takes 14 minutes. If the bottleneck is saturated, making the first product is the wrong choice. Most ERP systems and most quoting processes get this wrong.

The throughput-accounting view

Throughput (T) = Sales Price - Material Cost. Everything else (labor, overhead) is Operating Expense (OE) - largely fixed in the short term. The question is not "what is the margin" but "how much T does each minute of the bottleneck produce."

Then you maximize Net Profit = Total T - OE. Eliyahu Goldratt's Theory of Constraints, in a sentence.

Frequently asked questions

What is the bottleneck?

The resource (machine, work center, person) with the least excess capacity relative to demand. By definition, one of every production system. The whole shop's output is limited by it.

How do I find the bottleneck?

Walk the floor. Look for the resource with the most WIP in front of it, the highest utilization, the most frequent overtime, and the most expediting requests. It is usually obvious. If not, look at scheduling system load reports.

What if the bottleneck moves?

It will. As you exploit and elevate one bottleneck, another emerges. That is the normal cycle of Theory of Constraints work: identify, exploit, subordinate, elevate, repeat. Bottleneck-driven scheduling makes the shifts visible.

What does "subordinate" mean in TOC?

Non-bottleneck resources should not produce more than the bottleneck can consume. Producing ahead of the bottleneck builds WIP without throughput - waste, not productivity.

Should we always reject low-margin work?

Only if it consumes bottleneck time that could be filled by higher-throughput-per-minute work. If the bottleneck is below 80% utilization, take the low-margin work - it contributes throughput even at low margin per unit.
