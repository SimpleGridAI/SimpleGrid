Turns: the most important inventory metric

Inventory turns measures how many times per year your inventory sells through. A manufacturer with 4 turns holds 3 months of supply on average. With 12 turns: 1 month. Higher turns means less cash tied up, lower carrying cost, less obsolescence risk, more responsive operations.

How turns vary by industry

Food processing: 12-40 turns (perishables). Automotive parts: 8-30 (lean disciplines). General manufacturing: 6-10. Custom furniture: 4-7. Pick the right benchmark - "is my turns good" only makes sense within your vertical.

The cash math

Moving from 4 turns to 6 on a $3M inventory frees $1M in cash. Plus saves ~$250K/year in carrying cost (at 25% carrying rate). The lever for most manufacturers is real - and almost always under-pulled.

Frequently asked questions

How do I calculate inventory turns?

Annual COGS divided by average inventory value. Use the figure your accountant uses for COGS (not revenue - that overstates turns). Average inventory = (beginning + ending) / 2, or monthly average if available.

What is "days of supply"?

365 divided by turns. If turns is 6, days of supply is ~60. It is the average number of days a unit sits in inventory before being sold.

Is higher turns always better?

Mostly yes, but extreme turns can cause stockouts. Best-in-class manufacturers balance: high turns AND high service level. If your turns are 30 but your fill rate is 85%, you have a problem.

How fast can I improve turns?

20-30% improvement in 6-12 months is realistic for most manufacturers. The biggest sources: dead-stock cleanup (quick), batch-size reduction (medium), forecast improvement and lead-time reduction (slower, larger impact).

Should I track turns by category?

Yes. Raw materials, WIP, and finished goods have different dynamics. Tracking them separately reveals where the problem is - usually WIP for make-to-stock manufacturers, finished goods for those with poor demand forecasting.
