The quoting paradox in manufacturing

Most job shops quote everything that comes through the door. The estimating team treats every RFQ as equally worth working on. The result: 60% of their time goes to quotes with a 10% close rate, while the segments where they close at 40% get the same effort. The economics make no sense - but nobody can see them because nobody is tracking win rate by segment.

The single most important metric you are not tracking

Revenue per quote sent. Not win rate. Not average deal size. Revenue per quote sent = (revenue won from a segment) / (quotes sent to that segment). It captures both win rate and deal size in one number. Sort your segments by this metric and you see immediately which ones are worth your estimating team\'s time.

What to do with the data

Build a "quote acceptance policy" based on the data. For segments below your break-even quoting ROI, either raise the price (add a quoting fee for non-customers, raise minimums), tighten qualification (only quote referrals and repeat customers in this segment), or decline. Most shops never make this conscious decision; the estimating team just keeps grinding through whatever lands in the inbox.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good manufacturing win rate?

General manufacturing average is 25-30% (MAPI Foundation). Job shops run lower (15-25%) because they quote everything. Contract manufacturers with repeat customers run higher (35-50%). Best-in-class is 40%+.

How do I calculate the cost of a quote?

Estimator hours per quote times their loaded hourly rate. For mid-market manufacturing the typical range is $150-$800 per quote depending on complexity. Custom or engineered jobs run higher; standard catalog quotes run lower.

Why is revenue per quote sent more useful than win rate?

Win rate ignores deal size. A segment with 50% win rate and $5K average deal is worse than a segment with 25% win rate and $30K average deal. Revenue per quote sent combines both - it tells you the dollar return on each estimating hour spent.

Should I follow up on lost quotes?

Yes. MAPI research shows about 50% of manufacturers do not follow up at all. A simple 7-day and 14-day follow-up sequence increases win rate by 15-25% with no other changes.

How do I decline a quote politely?

Be honest: "This is below our minimum order size for cost reasons. We could quote it with a $X setup fee that brings the order to profitable - want me to send that?" Most prospects respect this. Some will pay the setup fee.
