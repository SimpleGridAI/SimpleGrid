Standard Time Calculator.

A defensible standard time has three ingredients: observed time, performance rating (was the operator working at normal pace?), and PFD allowance. Skip any one and your standard is wrong.

Time study

Performance rating: 100% = trained operator at normal pace. 80% = noticeably slow. 120% = fast/incentivized. Westinghouse system is the standard reference.

PFD allowance (Personal, Fatigue, Delay)

Standard total PFD: 10-15% for assembly, 15-25% for heavy physical work, 8-10% for seated light work. Reference: ILO and Niebel standards.

The three things a standard time must include

Observed time - what you saw with a stopwatch. Performance rating - was the operator working at normal pace, or sandbagging, or hustling? PFD allowance - what percentage of the day is unavoidably lost to breaks, fatigue, and short delays?

Skip the performance rating and you bake the observed operator's pace into the standard - rewarding slow workers and penalizing fast ones. Skip the PFD and your standard is unattainable - operators learn to ignore it. Both errors are common.

How many cycles?

Statistical theory says 5-15 cycles for short tasks (under a minute), 3-8 for longer tasks. Niebel's tables give exact numbers based on observed variability. In practice, 10 cycles is a good default for most factory work.

Frequently asked questions

What is performance rating?

A judgment by the time-study observer of how fast the worker was going. 100% = trained operator at normal pace. 90% = slightly slow. 110% = slightly fast. Westinghouse, Bedaux, and ILO have formal systems.

What PFD allowance should I use?

10-15% for assembly, 15-25% for heavy physical work, 8-10% for seated light work. Higher for hot environments, awkward postures, or high-precision tasks.

How many cycles do I need?

10 is a good default. For short repetitive tasks, 15-20. For long irregular tasks (10+ minutes), 3-5 may suffice. The narrower the cycle-to-cycle variation, the fewer cycles you need.

What if the operator was unusually fast or slow?

That is what performance rating is for. Rate at 110-120% if visibly fast; 80-90% if visibly slow. The math removes the operator-specific variation.

How does this work for non-repetitive jobs?

It does not, well. Standard time is for repetitive work. For job-shop or one-off work, use historical estimates or MTM/MOST (predetermined motion time systems).
