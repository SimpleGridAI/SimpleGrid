Why freight is the easiest line to misprice

You quote a customer a delivered price. Your shipping team books the freight. The number you assumed and the number that hits the books are different - sometimes by 30%. Multiply that across hundreds of orders a year and freight is suddenly the third-biggest line item on your P&L. Knowing your freight rates - even directionally - turns delivered pricing from a gamble into a calculation.

LTL vs FTL vs parcel

Parcel (UPS, FedEx ground): under 150 lbs per package, fixed per-package rates by zone. LTL (less than truckload): 150-15,000 lbs, palletized, priced by weight x class x distance. FTL (full truckload): 15,000+ lbs or 6+ pallets, priced per mile. The break-even between LTL and FTL is typically around 10,000-12,000 lbs depending on lane.

Freight class matters more than you think

NMFC freight class is determined by density (lbs per cubic foot). Class 50 (densest, 50+ pcf) is cheapest to ship. Class 500 (lightest, <1 pcf) is 4-5x more expensive. The same shipment value in steel sheet (class 60-70) ships for half the cost of the same value in foam (class 175+). Density matters - flat-pack, nest, or palletize tighter to drop class.

Frequently asked questions

How accurate is this estimate?

Within +/- 30% of market rates. Your actual carrier contracts can be 30-60% below market list. For binding numbers, get quotes from 2-3 carriers (or use an online freight aggregator: Freightos, Project44, etc.).

What is freight class?

A 1-500 number assigned by the NMFC (National Motor Freight Classification) based primarily on density (lbs/ft^3). Lower class = denser = cheaper to ship. Density of >50 pcf = Class 50 (cheapest). Density of <1 pcf = Class 500 (most expensive).

How do I lower my freight class?

Increase density: flat-pack, nest, palletize tighter, reduce dunnage, eliminate void space. A class 100 shipment becoming class 70 saves ~25% on LTL.

What is a fuel surcharge?

A separate line item carriers add to the base freight, indexed to diesel prices. Typically 25-32% of base freight. Fluctuates weekly with diesel index.

When should I switch from LTL to FTL?

When LTL cost approaches 80% of FTL cost. Typically around 10,000-12,000 lbs depending on lane and class. FTL also wins on transit time and handling damage risk.
