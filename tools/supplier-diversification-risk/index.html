Why concentrated supply chains looked smart until they did not

For 30 years the conventional wisdom was: consolidate to your best supplier, get volume discounts, simplify operations. COVID, the Suez Canal blockage, Section 301 tariffs, and Red Sea routing collapsed that thesis. Concentrated supply chains are now treated as a balance-sheet risk by sophisticated buyers - and increasingly, insurers and auditors are asking about it.

Three concentrations to watch

Supplier concentration. Sole-source items with no qualified alternative. Long qualification cycles make these systemic risks - you cannot replace fast enough to survive a disruption.

Geographic concentration. Too much spend in one country, one region, one port. A single trade dispute or weather event can paralyze a large chunk of your supply base.

Lead-time concentration. If 60% of your bill of materials sits in 12+ week lead-time items, your responsiveness to demand changes is destroyed by inventory commitments.

Frequently asked questions

What is sole-source spend?

Spend on items where you have only one qualified supplier. If that supplier fails, you cannot make the product. Most manufacturers underestimate their sole-source exposure - by spend, by criticality, and by qualification time required to recover.

How much sole-source spend is too much?

Below 15% of total spend: acceptable. 15-30%: monitor closely, plan diversification. Above 30%: high risk - any single supplier failure is a material event for the company.

What is qualification time?

How long it takes to identify a new supplier, run pilot/sample runs, perform quality audits, complete first-article inspection, and ramp to production volume. For commodity items: 1-3 months. For machined or engineered parts: 6-18 months. This is the actual recovery time if a sole-source fails.

Should I dual-source everything?

No - it adds complexity and cost. Focus on critical-criticality items and items with long qualification times. Standard items with 2-week lead time and 5 qualified suppliers are not the risk.

How much does it cost to qualify a backup supplier?

Time + sample/qual order cost + engineering review. Typically $3,000-$25,000 for standard parts, $50,000+ for engineered parts. Compared to the cost of a real disruption, this is a small insurance premium.
