Why most US manufacturers do not know their real duty exposure

Import duty is not a simple percentage. It is the regular MFN rate from the HTS schedule, plus Section 301 if the origin is China (7.5-25% extra), plus Section 232 if the product is steel or aluminum (10-25%), plus AD/CVD if the product is on a specific order (can be 100%+), minus any FTA preference. Most mid-market manufacturers know their MFN rate from a long-ago broker conversation. They do not know the current stack.

The Section 301 fact every manufacturer should know

Since 2018, most Chinese-origin manufacturing goods carry 7.5-25% in additional tariffs on top of the MFN rate. For a product with a 5% MFN rate, the total is 30%, not 5%. Many manufacturers built supplier relationships before 2018 and never re-evaluated when the math changed. The relationship feels established; the economics no longer work.

Mitigation strategies that actually work

Alternate-country sourcing (Vietnam, India, Mexico, Thailand for products that would otherwise come from China). USMCA preference for Mexico/Canada (verify rules of origin). FTZ or bonded warehouse to defer duty. Duty drawback if you re-export. None of these are exotic - they are standard tools used by sophisticated importers.

Frequently asked questions

What is Section 301?

The tariff added in 2018 on most Chinese-origin manufacturing imports. Rates 7.5-25% on top of regular MFN rate. Applied based on HTS chapter "Lists" 1-4. Subject to ongoing review - check ustr.gov for current status.

What is the difference between Section 232 and Section 301?

Section 232 = national security tariffs (steel, aluminum) - 10-25% on most origins, not just China. Section 301 = unfair trade practices (originally targeting China specifically) - 7.5-25% on most Chinese origin.

What is USMCA and how do I claim it?

US-Mexico-Canada Agreement. Replaces NAFTA. Products from Mexico/Canada qualify for duty-free entry if they meet the specific rule of origin for their HTS code (typically regional value content or substantial transformation). Your supplier provides a USMCA Certification of Origin.

Should I use an FTZ?

If you import substantial volume (>$500K annually) and have working capital constraints, yes. FTZ defers duty until goods leave the zone into US commerce. For high-duty products this materially improves cash flow.

What is duty drawback?

A refund of up to 99% of duties paid on imported materials that are used to make products subsequently exported. Must file within 5 years. Worth pursuing if you re-export more than ~$100K of duty per year.
