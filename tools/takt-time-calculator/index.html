Takt vs. cycle vs. lead time - three different things

Takt is the customer-driven pace. Cycle time is what your line actually produces. Lead time is order to delivery. They are often confused; they should not be.

The most common error: subtracting unplanned downtime from available time when calculating takt. Don't. Unplanned downtime is what OEE measures, and folding it into takt makes the number meaningless for planning.

When to recalculate

Whenever demand shifts more than 10-15%, whenever you change shift structure, whenever you add or drop a SKU that runs on the same line. Monthly recalc is typical for stable demand; weekly for seasonal businesses.

Frequently asked questions

What is takt time?

Takt is the maximum allowable time per unit to meet customer demand. Available production time divided by customer demand. If demand drops, takt rises (slower pace). If demand rises, takt falls (faster pace required).

How is takt different from cycle time?

Takt is set by the customer. Cycle time is what your line actually produces. The gap between them shows whether you have spare capacity or a deficit.

Should I subtract unplanned downtime?

No. Subtract planned breaks, lunch, scheduled maintenance, and shift-change losses. Unplanned downtime belongs in OEE, not takt. Folding it in makes the number meaningless for planning.

What if actual is faster than takt?

You have spare capacity. Either run fewer hours, take on additional work, or look for sandbagging - operators slowing down to fill the shift.

How often should we recalculate?

Whenever demand shifts 10-15% or you change shift structure or product mix. Monthly is typical; weekly for seasonal demand.
