Labor Efficiency Calculator.

Three different things: efficiency (earned vs. clocked-on time), utilization (clocked-on vs. paid time), productivity (earned vs. paid). Most managers confuse them. This tool separates them.

Time inputs (for the period)

Paid = total payroll hours including indirect time. Clocked-on = time logged against direct jobs. Earned = sum of (standard time x units produced) for all jobs completed.

Three metrics, three different things

Efficiency is how fast you ran when you were running. Utilization is how much of paid time was on direct work. Productivity is the product of both. Most managers confuse them and end up working on the wrong problem.

If efficiency is 95% but productivity is 60%, the problem is utilization - your operators are doing direct work fast, but they spend 40% of paid time on meetings, material handling, or waiting. You do not need faster operators; you need a smoother flow of work.

If utilization is 90% but efficiency is 75%, the problem is in the methods - standards may be stale, training inconsistent, or the actual work pace slower than expected. You do not need more discipline; you need to update standards or improve methods.

The hidden assumption: standards are right

All three metrics depend on standard times being accurate. If your standards have not been re-set in five years, the efficiency number is meaningless. Refresh standards every 1-2 years, or after any significant method change.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between efficiency, utilization, and productivity?

Efficiency = earned / clocked-on (how fast when running). Utilization = clocked-on / paid (how much time on direct work). Productivity = earned / paid (composite). They tell different stories - track them separately.

What is a good labor productivity number?

Mid-market manufacturers typically run 55-70%. Above 75% is strong. Above 85% is rare and usually indicates stale standards (too generous). Below 50% means significant lost time.

What is "indirect time"?

Time paid but not clocked against a direct job: meetings, training, material handling between jobs, equipment downtime, breaks beyond PFD. Some indirect time is necessary; the question is how much.

What if earned is higher than clocked?

Either standards are too generous (operators consistently beat them - re-set), or there is unrecorded direct work (operators not clocking onto small jobs). Investigate; do not celebrate.

How often should we refresh standards?

Every 1-2 years for stable products. After any method change (new fixture, layout, tool). Stale standards are the #1 cause of meaningless efficiency numbers.
