Why volume discounts often destroy margin

The typical conversation: customer asks for 15% off for a larger order. The sales rep, scared of losing the deal, says yes. Nobody calculates whether the unit cost actually drops 15% at that volume. Usually it does not - your unit cost drops 5-10% from setup amortization, and you just gave away the rest. Repeat this on every quote and you have a margin death spiral.

The right way to build discount tiers

Step 1: calculate your real cost curve. Setup cost divided by quantity, plus your variable cost (with labor efficiency factored in for longer runs). Step 2: split the cost savings - give the customer 60%, keep 40% as your margin reward for the bigger order. Step 3: cap discounts at your minimum margin floor. Anything below that is no longer a discount - it is a subsidy.

Why generous-but-bounded works

A 5% discount on a 5x larger order is far more profitable than a 0% discount on a 1x order. Customers feel rewarded; you make multiples more total profit. The tier structure also creates a buying signal - customers know exactly what they need to order to hit a better price, which moves them up the volume curve.

Frequently asked questions

How big should a volume discount be?

Cost-justified: give customers 50-70% of your unit-cost savings at higher volumes. If your cost drops 12% at 5x volume, a 6-8% discount is fair. Anything more is a giveaway; anything less leaves the customer feeling cheated.

Should every product have volume tiers?

Not necessarily. Tiers make sense when there is a real cost curve - setup amortization, labor efficiency, material bulk discounts. For products where unit cost is flat regardless of quantity, volume discounts are just giveaways.

How do I avoid customers gaming the tiers?

Set tiers based on order size, not annual volume. Or use blanket order structures where the customer commits to annual volume and you ship/invoice incrementally. Resist tier-stacking (cumulative discounts on top of quantity discounts) - it compounds quickly.

What is a margin floor?

The minimum acceptable gross margin you will accept on a unit, regardless of volume. For mid-market manufacturing, typical floors are 18-25%. Below that, the order does not cover SG&A and you lose money even when you "make a profit."

Why not just give the biggest customer the biggest discount?

Because customer concentration risk grows with discount depth - the bigger the discount, the more dependent you are on that customer staying. Better to keep discounts modest and broad than steep and concentrated.
