How to calculate true job cost in manufacturing

True job cost has four direct inputs and one indirect:

  • Direct materials - anything physically in the finished product. Include normal scrap allowance.
  • Direct labor - hours that touch the job, at the loaded rate (wage + taxes + benefits + worker's comp).
  • Machine time - hours on each machine at its full hourly rate (depreciation + power + tooling + maintenance).
  • Subcontractor / outside ops - heat treat, coating, plating, finishing done outside your shop.
  • Manufacturing overhead - facility, indirect labor, utilities, QC, applied as a % of direct labor + machine, or as a burden rate per direct labor hour.

Add all five = total job cost. Divide by units produced = per-unit cost. Subtract from selling price = gross margin.

What overhead rate should I use?

For US mid-market manufacturers, the typical overhead rate is 15–40 % of direct labor + machine. The honest way: take your trailing 12 months of indirect production costs (rent, utilities, indirect supervision, QC, depreciation, maintenance) and divide by direct labor + machine hours. Don't use industry averages - yours is probably different, and that difference matters when you quote a custom job.

FAQ

What's the difference between direct labor and indirect labor?

Direct = hands on the job (operator, assembler). Indirect = supervisor, scheduler, QC inspector, warehouse staff. Indirect labor is part of overhead, not direct labor.

How do I handle scrap and rework?

Two options: (1) include a 2–5 % scrap allowance in your material cost; OR (2) cost the job assuming 100% yield, then add a separate "rework / rejection" line. SimpleGrid's event-sourced costing handles this automatically - every rejection event posts the consumed cost to that job.

What's a healthy gross margin for manufacturing?

25–40 % gross margin (selling price minus total job cost, divided by selling price) is healthy for US mid-market discrete manufacturers. Below 15 % is usually unprofitable after you account for sales, admin, bad debt, and the cost of capital.
