EOQ in 2026

F.W. Harris published the EOQ formula in 1913. It is still the right starting point - sqrt(2 x demand x setup / holding). The cost curve is U-shaped and the bottom is flat: running 20-30% off EOQ usually adds less than 5% to total cost. That flatness is your degrees of freedom for prioritizing flow over cost.

Why lean operators run smaller than EOQ

Less WIP. Shorter lead time. Faster response to demand changes. Smaller quality scrap when something goes wrong. These benefits do not show up in the EOQ formula but they show up on the P&L. Reducing changeover time (SMED) lets you shrink batches without paying more in setup.

Frequently asked questions

Is EOQ still relevant?

Yes - as a starting point. Modern operators target smaller batches to improve flow, even at slightly above EOQ-minimum cost. EOQ tells you the cost penalty of going smaller.

What holding cost rate should I use?

20-30% per year is typical: capital cost (~8-10%), warehousing (~3-5%), obsolescence/spoilage (~5-10%), insurance/tax (~2%). Lower for stable parts; higher for perishables or tech.

What is EPEI?

Every Part Every Interval - how often a SKU comes around. EPEI of 5 days means every SKU runs at least once every 5 days. Lower EPEI = shorter lead times, less inventory.

How do I shrink batch size without raising cost?

Cut changeover time. Setup cost is the dominant lever. A 50% changeover cut lets you cut batch size by ~30% with no cost penalty.

What about quantity discounts?

EOQ does not include them. If a supplier offers a 5% discount at 2x EOQ, compare separately - the discount usually outweighs the holding cost increase if storage is cheap.
