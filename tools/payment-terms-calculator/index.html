Extended payment terms are a price discount in disguise

When a customer asks for Net 90 instead of Net 30, most manufacturers say yes without doing the math. The cost feels invisible - no line item on a P&L moves immediately. But the cost is real: you are financing your customer\'s operations with your own working capital, at your cost of capital. At 8% cost of capital, every 30 extra days of payment terms is a 0.66% price discount. Forever.

The early-pay discount trap

The classic "2/10 Net 30" early-pay discount sounds reasonable. The annualized cost is 36.5% APR. Most manufacturers offer it because "that is what is done." But you are giving customers 36.5% APR financing while you borrow at 8%. The math is upside-down.

Negotiation alternatives that protect your margin

Instead of "yes" or "no" to extended terms: (1) raise price by the cost differential, (2) tie the longer terms to a volume commitment, (3) offer tiered terms based on order size, (4) split the difference. Customers expect negotiation on payment terms - most have not been challenged before, so they ask reflexively. Show them the math; they often soften.

Frequently asked questions

How much do extended payment terms actually cost?

At 8% cost of capital, every extra 30 days of payment terms is equivalent to a 0.66% price discount on every order. Net 60 vs Net 30 = 0.66%. Net 90 vs Net 30 = 1.33%. These are recurring annual costs.

Should I offer an early-payment discount?

Usually no. The classic "2/10 Net 30" annualized = 36.5% APR. You can borrow capital cheaper than that almost anywhere. The only reason to offer early-pay discounts is if you genuinely cannot finance receivables and have no other source of working capital.

What is a fair price adjustment for extended terms?

Cost of capital times (extra days / 365). At 8% cost of capital, Net 60 instead of Net 30 = 30/365 x 8% = 0.66%. Round up to 1% for a clean number and to cover overhead of managing the longer terms.

How do I tell a customer their terms request will cost more?

Be matter-of-fact: "We can do Net 60. Our standard pricing assumes Net 30; on extended terms we have to adjust by about 1%. Want me to update the quote?" Most customers accept this - it is industry standard reasoning.

What if a customer just pays late regardless of terms?

Then your "Net 30" is actually Net 45 or Net 60 in practice. Calculate your real average days to pay and use that in your pricing. Some customers respond to late fees; most need a conversation.
