The number that exposes everything

Process cycle efficiency is the most damning metric in lean. It is value-add time divided by total lead time. Typical mid-market manufacturers run 1-5%. World-class hits 25%+. That gap - 95+% of lead time spent on non-value-add - is the biggest single opportunity in most factories.

The number is uncomfortable to see for the first time. A part that takes 8 minutes of actual work spends 16 hours on the floor. The customer paid for 8 minutes. They got 8 minutes. The other 15 hours and 52 minutes were waste.

Where the waste hides

Queue time, between value-add steps. Not in the machines. Not at the operators. In the wait. The lean countermeasure is not to speed up machines - it is to shrink the gaps between them.

Frequently asked questions

What is process cycle efficiency?

Value-add time divided by total lead time. The percentage of lead time that the customer would be willing to pay for if they could see your floor.

What is a good PCE?

World-class manufacturers: 25%+. Strong mid-market: 10-25%. Typical: 1-5%. Below 1% means a lot of queue time - the biggest improvement opportunity.

How do I tell value-add from non-value-add?

Three tests: (1) does it transform the product, (2) does the customer care, (3) is it done right the first time. If any answer is no, it is non-value-add. Inspection and rework fail test 1; transport fails test 2; scrap fails test 3.

Why is queue time so important?

In most factories, 80-95% of total lead time is queue. Reducing queue cuts lead time more than reducing cycle time ever will, and it is almost always cheaper.

When should we redraw the VSM?

Annually for stable products, semi-annually for high-mix. After any major process change (new machine, new product, layout change). And before any kaizen event to set the baseline.
