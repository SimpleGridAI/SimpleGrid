Why price increases feel scary - and why the math says otherwise

Most manufacturers raise prices reluctantly because they fear customer loss. The fear is real but usually misweighted. Here is the simple math: every dollar of price increase drops to the bottom line (no variable cost attached). Every dollar of volume loss only costs you the contribution margin (price minus variable cost). So at any price increase, you can lose meaningful volume and still come out ahead - because what you keep is worth more than what you lose.

The 5% / 15% rule

At typical manufacturing margins (25-35%), a 5% price increase survives a 15-20% volume loss before profit goes negative. That is your safety margin. In reality, B2B price elasticity is much lower than that - most manufacturers see less than 5% volume loss on a 5-7% increase (McKinsey B2B Pricing Study, 2021).

Why "doing nothing" is the most expensive option

Manufacturing input costs inflate at 3-5% a year on average. If you do not raise prices, your margin shrinks by exactly that amount each year. Over 3 years that is 10-15 margin points - more than the cost of any reasonable price increase. The choice is not "raise prices or do not." It is "raise prices proactively or watch margin erode silently."

Frequently asked questions

How much can I raise prices without losing customers?

B2B manufacturing customers are less price-sensitive than most owners think. Most data shows 3-7% increases produce under 5% volume loss when communicated properly (30-60 days notice, with value framing). Larger increases (10%+) can still work if costs justify them.

What is the break-even volume loss?

The point where the increased revenue per unit exactly offsets lost volume. Above this point, the price increase is net positive. The formula: break-even volume loss = (price increase / new contribution margin) at current profit.

Should I raise prices on all customers at once?

Usually no. Start with your least price-sensitive customers (longstanding, embedded, or growing accounts). Hold price-sensitive customers (commodity buyers, RFQ-driven) for a follow-up wave. This builds confidence and lets you adjust messaging.

How do I justify the increase to my customer?

Lead with value, not cost. "We have invested in capability X" beats "our costs went up." If you must reference cost, show the math: how much your costs rose, how much you absorbed, how much you are passing through. Customers respect transparency.

What if I have not raised prices in years?

You have a catch-up problem. The longer you go without raising prices, the larger the gap between your prices and inflation-adjusted reality. Catch up in stages (3-5% per year) rather than one large jump - easier to defend.
