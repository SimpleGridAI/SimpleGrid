The five velocity classes

Active (moved <30 days ago): your working stock - leave it alone. Normal (30-90): standard turnover. Slow (90-180): worth a look. Very slow (180-365): an alarm. Dead (>365): a museum exhibit costing you rent.

Why dead stock is the #1 hidden cost

Dead stock combines three problems: cash tied up that you cannot deploy, warehouse space consumed that you cannot use for active items, and write-off risk - the longer it sits, the less of its book value you will actually recover.

Disposition options (best to worst)

  1. Return to supplier (full or partial credit) - best outcome.
  2. Sell into another customer or product line - recover 100% of cost.
  3. Liquidate via surplus dealer or auction - recover 10-30%.
  4. Donate - tax deduction (consult accountant).
  5. Scrap - last resort.

Frequently asked questions

How often should I run this analysis?

Quarterly minimum. Monthly is best - the longer you wait, the lower your recovery rate.

What is "dead" - 6 months or 12?

Standard cutoff is 12 months for "dead." For perishable or technology products, dead can be 90 days. For commodity raw materials, 18-24 months can still be "very slow."

Can I get a tax deduction for write-offs?

Yes - inventory write-downs are deductible as ordinary business expense in the year recognized. Talk to your accountant about the documentation required.

Should I sell at a loss?

Almost always yes. A 70% loss today beats a 100% loss after the item becomes truly obsolete. Plus you free space and stop the carrying cost bleed.

How do I prevent dead stock in the first place?

Monthly slow-moving reports (90-day threshold), engineering change alerts that flag affected inventory, restocking fees on cancelled custom orders, and lower MOQs negotiated with suppliers for slow movers.
