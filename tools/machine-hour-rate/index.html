Why a flat shop rate guarantees mis-pricing

Most US machine shops quote at a single $65-95/hr rate across all equipment. The new 5-axis with the $400k price tag and the 30-year-old Bridgeport get the same rate. Result: you underprice work on the new machine and overprice work on the old one - and you win business on what loses money while losing business on what should win.

The fix is not complicated. Each machine has its own capital cost, energy draw, operator coverage, and maintenance profile. Sum them, divide by productive hours, and you have a defensible rate. Lump them and you do not.

The interest-on-capital question

Should you include interest on capital? Yes - even if you paid cash. The cash could be earning return elsewhere. Use your WACC or the rate you would pay for a new machine loan. Excluding it understates the true cost of holding capital equipment.

Frequently asked questions

How do I pick utilization?

Use actual run-time hours, not scheduled hours. 65% (one shift, busy CNC) is typical. 85% means running close to 3 shifts. Below 50% means structurally over-bought.

Should I include interest on capital?

Yes - even if you paid cash. The cash could be earning return elsewhere. Use WACC or the rate you would pay for a new machine loan.

What about operator coverage?

A 5-axis with pallet changer might run 60% unattended - operator coverage 0.4. A manual lathe needs full attention - 1.0. Adjust per machine.

Why does this differ from accounting numbers?

Accounting uses sunk-cost depreciation (often 7-year MACRS). This uses useful-economic-life depreciation, which is more honest for pricing decisions. Difference can be $10-15/hr on capital-intensive machines.

Should every machine have its own rate?

Yes if rates differ by more than 20%. Lumping a $50k VMC with a $400k 5-axis under one shop rate guarantees mis-pricing on both.
