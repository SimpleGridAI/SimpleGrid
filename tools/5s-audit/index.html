5S Audit Scorecard.

A consistent 5S score across all areas of the shop is one of the strongest predictors of overall operational health. This tool scores all five S categories and produces a printable audit.

Area

Sort (Seiri) - eliminate unnecessary items

Set in order (Seiton) - a place for everything

Shine (Seiso) - clean and inspect

Standardize (Seiketsu) - consistent procedures

Sustain (Shitsuke) - discipline and audit cadence

Why 5S is the leading indicator

The strongest predictor of operational health in a manufacturing facility is not OEE, not on-time delivery, not defect rate. It is the consistency of the 5S score across areas. Areas that score 80%+ have lower turnover, fewer safety incidents, faster training cycles, and shorter setups. Areas that score below 50% have all the operational problems you would expect.

5S is not housekeeping. It is the operating system underneath everything else. A messy area is symptomatic of unclear standards, weak accountability, and absent management presence - all of which show up in cost and quality numbers.

Where most 5S programs fail

Sustain. The first four S's are one-time events. Sustain is the muscle that keeps them in place when the consultant leaves. If the audit cadence is not on a calendar, if findings do not have owners and dates, if leaders do not walk the area, 5S degrades within months.

Frequently asked questions

What does 5S stand for?

Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain. From Japanese: Seiri, Seiton, Seiso, Seiketsu, Shitsuke. Originated at Toyota.

How often should we audit?

Weekly for areas under 70%. Bi-weekly for 70-85%. Monthly for 85%+. The cadence should ratchet down only after the area has held its score for 90 days.

Who should do the audit?

Mixed: someone from the area + someone from outside. Insiders know the standards; outsiders see what insiders no longer notice. Rotate quarterly.

What score should we target?

85%+ is world-class. 70-85% is strong mid-market. Below 55% needs a kaizen blitz before continuing audit cadence.

Is 5S worth doing in a job shop?

Yes - even more than in repetitive manufacturing. High-mix work depends on operators finding the right tools and materials quickly. 5S pays back fastest in job shops.
