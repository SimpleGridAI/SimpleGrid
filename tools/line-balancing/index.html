Why most lines are imbalanced

Lines get balanced once - at install. Then a method changes, a tool moves, a station gets a second monitor for "kitting." Within six months, one station is the bottleneck and three stations are waiting for it. The line runs at 70% efficiency and nobody can quite say why.

The first sign: high WIP at one station, idle operators at others. The second sign: overtime that does not correlate with demand. The third sign: complaints about a specific station from the operators rotating into it.

What good balancing looks like

90%+ balance efficiency. Station loads within 10% of each other. Visible work distribution so a supervisor can see at a glance who is keeping up. And periodic rebalancing - quarterly is typical for stable products, monthly for high-mix.

Frequently asked questions

What is line balancing?

Assigning tasks to workstations so each has roughly equal work time, close to but under takt. Good balance = high efficiency and low idle time.

Why does the longest task matter?

It sets the floor for cycle time. If one task takes 90 seconds and takt is 60 seconds, you cannot meet takt with a single-line layout. You must split the task or run parallel stations.

What is PFD allowance?

Personal, Fatigue, and Delay - the percentage of time operators lose to micro-stops and fatigue. 10-15% is standard for assembly; 8-10% for seated/light work.

What balance efficiency should I target?

Above 90% is excellent. 80-90% is typical for well-run mid-market lines. Below 75% means rebalancing will pay back fast.

Does this handle task precedence?

No - it uses the Largest Candidate Rule. For assemblies with strict precedence, verify the output respects your precedence diagram by hand.
