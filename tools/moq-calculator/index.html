Why your sales team accepts jobs that lose money

Setup costs are invisible. They show up as overhead on the P&L, not as a line item on the job. So when a 25-piece order comes in, nobody asks "did we cover setup?" - they ask "what is our unit cost?" And the unit cost answer ignores the 2 hours of changeover, the first-article inspection, the test pieces in the scrap bin, and the order-entry admin. By the time you finish the job, you have lost money on it. Multiply that by 50-100 small orders a year and the leak is real.

Three MOQs to know

  • Break-even MOQ - the quantity where total revenue equals total cost. Below this you lose money on the job, no matter how favorable your unit-cost math looks.
  • Target margin MOQ - the quantity where the job hits your target gross margin (typically 25-35% for mid-market manufacturing).
  • Sweet spot MOQ - the quantity where adding more volume barely improves your margin (less than 1 percentage point per added unit). Beyond this you have already amortized setup; you are basically running variable cost.

The setup-fee fix

If your break-even MOQ is above 50 units, you are almost certainly losing money on small orders. The fix is not "don\'t take small orders" - it is a setup fee that covers most of your setup cost. Charging a setup fee on small runs is standard practice in job shops, CNC, and sheet-metal fab. It is not seen as rude by experienced buyers; it is seen as professional.

Frequently asked questions

What is a minimum order quantity (MOQ)?

The smallest order you can take and still cover your setup, changeover, and overhead costs at an acceptable margin. Below your MOQ, the per-unit cost of setup is so high that the job loses money.

How do I know my real MOQ?

Add up everything that happens once per job regardless of quantity (setup labor, programming, tooling, first-article inspection, admin) - that is your setup cost. Divide it by your contribution margin per unit (price minus variable cost). That is your break-even MOQ.

Should I refuse small orders?

Usually not. Refuse to lose money on them. Add a setup fee on orders below your break-even MOQ. Most experienced buyers expect this on small runs and pay without complaint.

How big should my setup fee be?

Cover 50-80% of your setup cost. A 100% setup-fee makes small orders financially equivalent to large ones - that is the most defensible position. But 50% is usually enough to stop losing money.

Why do I lose money on small orders even though my unit cost looks fine?

Because your unit cost was calculated assuming full-run amortization of setup. On a 25-piece order, setup has to be amortized across 25 pieces instead of 500 - which multiplies the setup cost per unit by 20x.
