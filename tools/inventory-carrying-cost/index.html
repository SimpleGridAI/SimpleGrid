The four buckets of inventory carrying cost

Capital. The opportunity cost of cash tied up in inventory. Usually the largest single component (40-60% of carrying cost). Calculate as inventory value times your cost of capital or borrowing rate.

Storage. Warehouse rent, utilities, racking, forklifts, warehouse labor allocated to storage. Manufacturers underestimate this because labor is often hidden in other budgets.

Risk. Obsolescence (3-5% typical), shrinkage (0.5-2%), damage (0.5%). The risk number rises during periods of engineering change, product mix shift, or supplier consolidation.

Service. Insurance (0.3-0.5%), property tax on inventory (varies by state), cycle counting labor, inventory management systems. Small individually but real in aggregate.

The reduction math

Every 1% reduction in inventory at typical carrying rates (~25%) saves 0.25% of inventory value per year, plus frees that 1% in working capital. For a $3M inventory: a 10% reduction frees $300K and saves $75K/year - the kind of move you would make at the board level.

Frequently asked questions

What is a typical carrying cost rate?

Manufacturing median is 22-28%. Top-quartile companies operate at 15-18%. Below 15% is unusual and usually means the calculation is incomplete. Above 35% suggests high obsolescence or very expensive capital.

What goes in "cost of capital"?

For most mid-market manufacturers: your line-of-credit rate (typically 6-10%) or your WACC. If you are sitting on cash, use the return you could earn elsewhere (long-term Treasury rate, ~4-5%, is a conservative floor).

How do I estimate obsolescence rate?

Look at your write-offs over the last 3 years as a % of average inventory. If you do not track that, manufacturing average is 2-5%. Stable commodities: 1-2%. Engineered or fashion-sensitive products: 5-10%.

Why is capital cost usually the biggest component?

Because manufacturers carry a lot of inventory. At 8% cost of capital and $1M inventory, capital cost alone is $80K - usually more than rent, insurance, and damage combined.

Should I include freight in carrying cost?

No - freight is acquisition cost (part of landed cost or COGS), not carrying cost. Carrying cost only covers the cost of HOLDING inventory once it is yours.
