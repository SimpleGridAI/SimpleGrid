The 80/20 rule applies to your inventory

Vilfredo Pareto observed in 1896 that 80% of land in Italy was owned by 20% of the population. The pattern repeats everywhere - including your warehouse. Roughly 20% of your SKUs drive 80% of your inventory spend. Those are your A items. The bottom 50% drive maybe 5% of spend. Managing them the same way is operational malpractice.

Why this matters in cash terms

A items deserve weekly attention because they hold most of your inventory cash. Improving turns on A items by 10% frees real money. C items are noise - over-investing attention on them is wasted effort that should be redirected to A items where the leverage is.

Original framework

H. Ford Dickie at GE formalized ABC classification in 1951. APICS/ASCM adopted it as the foundation of inventory management. The advanced version (ABC-XYZ) adds a second dimension for demand predictability - useful once you have monthly usage data, but the basic ABC is the right place to start.

Frequently asked questions

What thresholds should I use for A/B/C?

Classic 80/15/5 is the right starting point. If your top items are very concentrated (top 10 items = 80% of value), shift to 70/20/10. The point is to identify a small, manageable A list - not to match any particular textbook split.

How often should I re-run ABC?

Quarterly is standard. After major changes (new product launch, customer loss, supplier change) re-run immediately - the classifications can shift fast.

Should I include WIP and finished goods?

For raw material inventory management, just raw materials. For overall portfolio analysis, all categories. Run them separately - their dynamics are different.

What is ABC-XYZ?

A 9-box matrix combining ABC (value) with XYZ (demand predictability). AX = high-value/stable (great for JIT). AZ = high-value/erratic (the most dangerous box - tight monitoring required). Requires 12+ months of monthly usage data to compute.

Should I delete C items?

Audit them, do not auto-delete. Items with zero usage in 12 months are dead stock candidates. Items with low but consistent usage may be necessary spare parts or seasonal items - simplify their management but keep them.
