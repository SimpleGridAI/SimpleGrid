Why "we are out of space" is usually wrong

WERC benchmarks show most mid-market warehouses run at 65-75% storage utilization. That means 25-35% of the space you are paying for is wasted - wide aisles, empty rack positions, dead stock, poor slotting. Before you sign a lease on a second building, find out where the space is actually going.

The three quickest wins

Vertical. If your clear height supports more rack levels than you are using, you can add capacity at the cost of racking only - no floor expansion.

Dead stock. 10-20% of warehouse space typically holds inventory that has not moved in 12+ months. Liquidating it frees space AND cash.

Aisle narrowing. Counterbalance forklifts need 12-ft aisles; reach trucks need 9 ft; narrow-aisle trucks need 6 ft. Switching equipment can reclaim 20-30% of aisle space.

Frequently asked questions

What is "good" storage utilization?

85-90% is healthy. Above 95% means you have no buffer - one big receipt and you cannot put it away. Below 70% is over-built and expensive.

Should I narrow my aisles?

If counterbalance forklift aisles take >25% of your floor, yes. The forklift swap pays back in 12-24 months at typical rents. If you only have a manual pallet jack, no - you already have narrow aisles.

How much vertical space am I wasting?

If your rack height is less than (clear height - 2 ft), you are leaving capacity on the table. Each additional rack level adds ~33% more pallet positions per square foot of floor.

When should I lease more space?

When storage utilization is >90% AFTER you have cleared dead stock and maximized vertical. Before that, you are leasing for inefficiency, not for need.

What is dead-stock space cost?

At $9/sq ft and 15% dead-stock occupancy in a 22,000 sq ft warehouse, dead stock alone is consuming ~3,300 sq ft = $30K/year in rent. Plus what is on those shelves is depreciating.
