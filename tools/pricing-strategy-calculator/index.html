Why pricing is the most under-analyzed lever in manufacturing

A 1% price improvement increases operating profit by 8-11% for the average manufacturer (McKinsey 2019) - more than a 1% reduction in variable cost, more than a 1% reduction in fixed cost, more than a 1% increase in volume. Yet pricing is the least analyzed decision in most shops. Most owners use cost-plus because it is safe. Safe pricing leaves money on the table.

The three pricing methods, in one paragraph each

Cost-plus. Add a markup to your cost. Simple. Ignores what customers will pay and what competitors charge. The default in manufacturing because it feels rational, but it is the floor, not the right price.

Competitive. Price relative to the market. Useful when products are comparable. Fails when your product or service is better than the market average - you leave value on the table.

Value-based. Price by what the customer will pay based on the value you deliver. Considers quality, delivery speed, customization, switching cost, and reputation. Hardest to do; biggest upside. Simon-Kucher research shows manufacturers who move to value-based pricing improve margins by 2-7 percentage points.

When to use which

Use cost-plus only as a floor check ("am I above cost?"). Use competitive when you sell a true commodity with no differentiation. Use value-based everywhere else - which is most products, for most manufacturers.

Frequently asked questions

What is value-based pricing in manufacturing?

Value-based pricing means setting prices by what the customer would willingly pay based on the value you deliver - faster delivery, higher quality, customization, lower switching cost, brand reputation - rather than by your cost plus a markup. Simon-Kucher data shows it adds 2-7 margin points vs. cost-plus.

How do I know if I am underpricing my products?

Signs you are underpricing: customers never push back on price; you win an unusually high share of quotes (over 50% with no follow-up); competitor prices are well above yours despite comparable product; your margin has shrunk over the last 3 years as costs rose but you did not pass through.

What is a good margin for a manufacturing business?

Gross margin (per-product): 25-40% is typical for mid-market manufacturing. Below 20% is fragile - SG&A and bad debt will eat it. EBITDA margin (whole business): manufacturing median is 8-12% (Census Bureau ASM); top quartile is 16%+.

Should I use cost-plus or value-based pricing?

Use cost-plus only as a floor check ("am I above cost?"). For everything else, use value-based. Cost-plus is safe but it gives you the worst margins of any method because it ignores what customers will pay.

How does delivery speed affect pricing power?

For most manufacturing categories, customers will pay 5-15% more for faster delivery. The premium depends on how time-critical their use is. JIT customers, seasonal businesses, and project-driven buyers value speed most.
