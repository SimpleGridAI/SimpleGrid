Why your biggest customer is not always your most profitable

The "whale curve" analysis (Robert Kaplan, HBR) consistently shows: in most B2B businesses, 20% of customers generate 150-300% of profits, while the bottom 20% destroy 50-100% of those profits. The net is positive, but you would make more money with fewer customers if you knew which to keep. The problem: most manufacturers track revenue, not cost-to-serve. Revenue tells you who is buying; profitability tells you who is paying for itself.

The five hidden costs that eat customer profit

  1. Payment financing. A customer on Net 60 instead of Net 30 costs you 30 days of capital tied up.
  2. Returns & quality issues. Each return costs the unit cost plus handling, restocking, replacement freight.
  3. Special handling. Custom packaging, dedicated inventory, consignment - all real cost.
  4. Support & account management. Some customers call weekly; others call once a quarter.
  5. Quality audits & certifications. Customer-required audits, qualification runs, certification maintenance.

What to do with the data

Stars: protect them, ask for referrals. Profitable: maintain. Marginal: address root causes (returns, payment terms, special handling). Loss-makers: raise prices first (they are usually not your most price-sensitive), reduce service level second, exit third. The data gives you permission to have hard conversations with customers who are quietly costing you money.

Frequently asked questions

What is cost-to-serve?

All the costs you incur to keep a customer relationship running beyond the cost of goods - payment financing (slow payers), returns and quality issues, special handling and customization, support hours, audits, freight you bear. For most manufacturers it is 5-20% of revenue per customer.

How much profit do unprofitable customers actually destroy?

Bain/Kaplan research: the bottom 20% of B2B customers typically destroy 50-100% of the profit generated by the top 20%. The net is positive but reduced. Removing or repricing the bottom 20% usually increases total profit.

Should I fire unprofitable customers?

Usually not as the first step. Raise their prices first - they are rarely your most price-sensitive. Reduce service level second (standard packaging, standard terms). Charge for currently-free services third. Only exit when all of those fail.

How do I calculate cost-to-serve quickly?

Start with the four biggest categories: payment financing (excess AR days x cost of capital), returns (return rate x revenue x 1.5 for handling overhead), special handling (1-6% of revenue depending on level), support hours (monthly hours x 12 x loaded rate).

What is the whale curve?

A chart of cumulative profit by customer, sorted most-profitable to least. Profits rise steeply from the top customers, plateau, then DECLINE as unprofitable customers erode profit. The peak shows where you stop making money - everyone after that point is destroying value.
