Shingo's insight

Most "setup" is not setup. Walking to the tool crib, finding the right bolts, fetching the next die, writing up the job - these don't need the machine down. They get done while the machine is down because nobody has separated them.

Once you separate them, you can do them in parallel with the prior run. The machine sits idle for less time. The line throughput goes up by exactly the amount you externalize. Most operators are surprised by how much this is - typically 30-50% on the first pass.

Why "single-minute" matters

The original SMED goal: single-digit minutes (under 10 minutes). Why? Below 10 minutes, you can run smaller batches without losing capacity to changeover. Smaller batches = less WIP, shorter lead time, faster response to demand. SMED is the enabling step for everything else in lean.

Frequently asked questions

What does SMED stand for?

Single-Minute Exchange of Die. Coined by Shigeo Shingo at Toyota. The original target: sub-10-minute changeovers (single digits of minutes), not literally 60 seconds.

How do I tell internal from external?

Internal = the machine must be down for this step (e.g., physically swapping the die). External = could be done while the previous job is still running (e.g., walking to fetch the next die, pre-heating, paperwork).

What is a realistic improvement?

First-pass workshops cut changeover by 40-60% just by externalizing prep work. Another 20-30% comes from parallel operations and fixturing over the following months.

Do we need to invest in tooling?

Not for Stage 1 (separate internal vs. external) - pure process. Stage 2 sometimes needs preset jigs or duplicate fixtures. Stage 3 (streamline remaining internal) is where capex shows up.

Which lines benefit most?

High-mix, low-volume lines with frequent changeovers - machine shops, packaging lines, short-run fab. If you change over more than twice per shift, SMED pays back in weeks.
