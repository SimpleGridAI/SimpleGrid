Little's Law is not negotiable

In any stable production system, WIP equals throughput multiplied by lead time. It is a steady-state identity, not an approximation. You cannot have low WIP and long lead time. You cannot have high throughput and tiny WIP unless lead time is short.

That means if you want shorter lead time, you must reduce WIP. And reducing WIP without losing throughput requires cutting queue time - the time material spends waiting between value-add operations. In most manufacturers, queue time is 80-95% of total lead time. There is enormous room to compress it.

What WIP includes

Anything between raw material release and finished goods. Parts in machines, parts in transit between stations, parts waiting in queues, parts in inspection. Raw material and finished goods are tracked separately - they have different dynamics.

Frequently asked questions

What is Little's Law?

WIP = Throughput x Lead Time. A foundational equation in queueing theory and factory physics. It always holds in steady-state systems, regardless of variability.

How can I cut lead time without losing throughput?

Reduce queue time. Smaller batches, fewer buffers, pull scheduling, smoothed demand. Capacity is rarely the binding constraint - queue time usually is.

What counts as WIP?

Anything between raw material release and finished goods. Parts in machines, in transit, in queues, in inspection. Raw material and finished goods are separate.

Is shorter lead time always better?

Mostly yes. Faster response to demand, earlier quality detection, less working capital. Exception: capital-intensive batch processes (heat treat, paint) need some buffer.

How fast can WIP be reduced?

30-50% reduction in 90 days is realistic for most discrete manufacturers if you reduce batch sizes, eliminate unnecessary inspections, and pull schedule. Faster reductions usually create stockouts.
