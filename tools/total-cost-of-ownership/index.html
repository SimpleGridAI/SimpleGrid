Why the $2 part costs $3.40

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) goes beyond purchase price to include every cost associated with acquiring, receiving, storing, and using a material. For overseas-sourced items, TCO is typically 30-50% higher than the quoted price. For domestic items, 10-20% higher. Most procurement decisions are made on quoted price - which is why most procurement decisions are wrong.

The five buckets of hidden cost

  1. Logistics. Freight, duties, brokerage, port handling, insurance. For overseas shipments: 15-25% of purchase cost.
  2. Quality. Receiving inspection, rejects, returns, replacement delays, rework. For an unreliable supplier: 5-15% of purchase cost.
  3. Inventory. Carrying cost (storage, insurance, obsolescence, capital cost) on safety stock and cycle stock. Long lead times multiply this.
  4. Risk. Production stoppages from supply disruptions, admin time, engineering support hours.
  5. Financing. Payment terms create cost or benefit vs. the standard Net 30 baseline.

Methodology

Based on Dr. Lisa Ellram's 1993 TCO framework (the foundational academic work, widely adopted by CAPS Research and the Institute for Supply Management). The tool decomposes cost into six categories and computes total cost across all of them, normalized to TCO per unit and TCO as a percentage premium over quoted price.

Frequently asked questions

What is a typical TCO premium over quoted price?

Domestic suppliers with strong quality: 10-15% above quoted price. Overseas suppliers (China, Vietnam, India): 30-50%. The premium is driven mostly by freight, duties, inspection, and the inventory cost of long lead times.

How accurate is this TCO calculation?

Directionally accurate within +/- 15% if the inputs are honest. The biggest sources of error are reject rate (often under-reported), disruption cost (often estimated too low), and admin time (almost never tracked).

Why does payment terms show as a benefit?

Terms longer than 30 days are essentially free financing from the supplier - your supplier carries the receivable instead of you carrying the payable. At an 8% cost of capital, every 30 days of terms beyond Net 30 is worth ~0.66% of purchase cost.

Should I always pick the lowest TCO supplier?

Usually yes, but consider concentration risk (do not put 100% with one supplier even if their TCO is best) and qualification status (a lower-TCO unqualified supplier is not actually available to you).

How does this differ from landed cost?

Landed cost is one slice of TCO - the freight + duties + clearance + delivery portion. TCO adds quality, inventory, risk, and financing. Landed cost answers "what does it cost to get it here?" - TCO answers "what does it cost to own it across a year of use?"
